Introduction to Equilibrium

  • Equilibrium: A state of balance in a system, where opposing forces or processes are in balance.

  • At equilibrium, the system’s state does not change over time: \(\frac{d}{dt} f(t) = 0\).

  • Stable Equilibrium: A system returns to its equilibrium state after a small perturbation.

  • Unstable Equilibrium: A system moves away from its equilibrium state after a small perturbation.

Stable Equilibrium Example

  • Cooling or Heating Law (Newton’s Law of Cooling)

Equation: \(\frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_{\text{env}})\), where \(T\) is the temperature of the object, \(T_{\text{env}}\) is the ambient (environmental) temperature, and \(k\) is a constant related to the cooling or heating efficiency.

  • Application: Describes how the temperature of an object changes in a cooler or warmer environment.

  • Exact Solution: \(T(t) = T_{\text{env}} + (T(0) - T_{\text{env}})e^{kt}\), where \(T(0)\) is the initial temperature of the object.

  • Example: A cup of coffee cools in a room. If the room temperature is 20°C and the coffee initially is 80°C, the coffee’s temperature over time can be modeled with this equation.

Unstable Equilibrium Example

  • Exponential Growth or Decay

Equation: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = ky\), where \(k\) is a constant.

  • Application: Models natural phenomena like population growth (if \(k > 0\)) or radioactive decay (if \(k < 0\)).

  • Exact Solution: \(y(x) = y(0)e^{kx}\), where \(y(0)\) is the initial condition.

  • Example: If a bacteria population doubles every hour, the rate \(k\) is \(\ln(2)\), and the population at any time \(x\) can be described by \(y(x) = y(0)e^{\ln(2)x}\).

  • Simple RC Circuit (Charging a Capacitor)

Equation: \(\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{1}{RC}(V_{\text{source}} - V)\), where \(V\) is the voltage across the capacitor, \(V_{\text{source}}\) is the source voltage, \(R\) is the resistance, and \(C\) is the capacitance.

  • Application: Models the voltage change over a capacitor as it charges from a voltage source through a resistor.

  • Exact Solution: \(V(t) = V_{\text{source}} - (V_{\text{source}} - V(0))e^{-\frac{t}{RC}}\), where \(V(0)\) is the initial voltage across the capacitor.

  • Example: If a 1V capacitor charges through a 1Ω resistor from a 5V source, the voltage across the capacitor over time can be described with this equation.
