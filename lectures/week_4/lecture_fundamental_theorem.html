The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

  • Links differentiation of a function to the definite integral of a function.

  • Differentiation and integration are inverse operations.

Differentiation is anti-integration

  • If \(F(x)\) is an antiderivative of \(f(x)\), then the definite integral of \(f(x)\) from \(a\) to \(b\) is equal to \(F(b) - F(a)\). Let \(F'(x) = f(x)\), then:

\[\int_a^b f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)\]

  • This means that the process of integration can be “reversed” by differentiation.

Integration is anti-differentiation

  • If \(f\) is a continuous function on the interval \([a, b]\) and \(F\) is defined by the integral:

    \[F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) dt\]

    then \(F\) is continuously differentiable on \([a, b]\), and \(F'(x) = f(x)\) for every \(x\) in \([a, b]\).

  • This means that the process of differentiation can be reversed by integration (up to a constant).
